Your Students Are Learning. Just Not to Speak.

Traditional grammar-focused instruction routes language knowledge into the wrong brain system — one that cannot produce fluent speech. Four decades of converging evidence from neurolinguistics, cognitive science, and classroom research demonstrate that explicit grammar teaching builds declarative memory representations (facts about language) rather than the procedural memory competence (automatic language use) that underlies all fluent communication. Meanwhile, acquisition-based methods grounded in comprehensible input consistently produce equal or superior outcomes in controlled studies, with particular advantages in fluency, long-term retention, and student motivation.

Part I

The case against grammar-focused instruction

Two brain systems, two kinds of knowledge — and only one produces speech

The strongest scientific argument against grammar instruction as a path to fluency comes from neurolinguistics. Michel Paradis (2004, 2009), drawing on 25+ years of bilingual aphasia data, neuroimaging, and neurological pathology, demonstrated that implicit linguistic competence and explicit metalinguistic knowledge are stored in fundamentally different brain systems. Implicit competence — the kind that enables fluent, automatic speech — relies on procedural memory, subserved by the basal ganglia, cerebellum, and left perisylvian cortex. Explicit grammar knowledge — the kind produced by classroom instruction — relies on declarative memory, subserved by the hippocampus and medial temporal lobes.

"Metalinguistic knowledge never becomes implicit competence, or the other way around."— Michel Paradis (2004, 2009)

Paradis's critical conclusion is unequivocal. The two systems are doubly dissociated in brain pathology. Patients with amnesia (impaired declarative memory) retain all implicit linguistic competence. Patients with aphasia (impaired procedural language circuits) lose speech production but retain explicit grammar knowledge. Parkinson's disease patients show degraded procedural grammar; Alzheimer's patients lose declarative knowledge first — and notably, late-learned L2 has been reported to deteriorate early in Alzheimer's, suggesting it was primarily stored in declarative memory.

Michael Ullman's Declarative/Procedural (DP) Model (2001, 2004, 2016) extends this framework with detailed neurocognitive mapping. In native speakers, grammar processing depends on procedural memory circuits (Broca's area/BA 44, caudate, putamen). In L2 learners taught explicitly, grammar is initially processed through declarative memory — a fundamentally different computational substrate.

A landmark neuroimaging meta-analysis by Tagarelli, Shattuck, Turkeltaub, and Ullman (2019, NeuroImage) confirmed this directly: grammar learning with explicit training activated medial temporal lobe (declarative) regions, whereas grammar learning without explicit instruction activated basal ganglia (procedural) regions. The type of instruction literally determined which brain system was recruited.

Can declarative grammar knowledge eventually "convert" to procedural competence? Hamrick, Lum, and Ullman (2018, PNAS) conducted multiple meta-analyses showing that L2 grammar was associated with only declarative memory at lower experience levels but with only procedural memory at higher experience levels. This shift happens — but through extensive communicative use, not through more grammar practice. Paradis (2009) clarifies that what occurs is not conversion but substitution: implicit competence is separately and incidentally acquired through communicative use, and its use gradually replaces reliance on explicit knowledge. Grammar instruction cannot accelerate this process because it feeds the wrong system.

For K-12 learners, this evidence is especially consequential. Children and adolescents still have strong access to procedural memory for language learning. Pushing grammar instruction on school-age learners channels acquisition into the declarative system precisely when the procedural system is most available — wasting a developmental window that closes with age.

Morgan-Short's experiments: same test scores, radically different brains

The most direct experimental evidence comes from Kara Morgan-Short and colleagues (2012, Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience). Adults learned an artificial language under either explicit training (grammar rules explained, approximating classroom instruction) or implicit training (immersion-like, no rules given). At high proficiency, both groups performed identically on behavioral tests. But ERP brain recordings told a starkly different story.

"Only implicit training led to an electrophysiological signature typical of native speakers."— Morgan-Short et al. (2012)

Only the implicitly trained group showed the full native-speaker brain signature — an anterior negativity followed by a P600, the pattern characteristic of automatic syntactic processing. The explicitly trained group showed an anterior positivity followed by a P600 — a pattern reflecting reliance on domain-general attentional control mechanisms rather than native-like syntactic processing.

A follow-up retention study (Morgan-Short, Finger, Grey, & Ullman, 2012, PLoS ONE) found that after months of no exposure, the implicitly trained group still showed more native-like processing than the explicitly trained group. Training type has lasting neural consequences.

Complementary evidence from Bowden, Steinhauer, Sanz, and Ullman (2013, Neuropsychologia) showed that university L2 Spanish learners achieved native-like brain responses — but only those who had immersion experience beyond classroom instruction. Williams (2020, Language Learning) confirmed a competitive relationship between declarative and procedural learning systems, consistent with the prediction that explicit grammar instruction can actively inhibit procedural learning.

Krashen's framework: theoretical foundations validated by neuroscience

Stephen Krashen's Monitor Model (1981, 1982, 1985) anticipated these neurolinguistic findings by two decades. His Acquisition-Learning Distinction proposed that "acquired" language (subconscious, meaning-focused) and "learned" language (conscious grammar rules) are fundamentally separate systems. His Monitor Hypothesis specified that learned knowledge can only function as a post-production editor, operating only when the speaker knows the rule, has time to apply it, and is focused on correctness.

Krashen identified "Monitor over-users" — typically perfectionistic students produced by grammar-heavy classrooms — who "produce slowly, inadequately but correctly" because their learned rules interfere with fluent output. The neurolinguistic evidence from Paradis, Ullman, and Morgan-Short now provides independent neurobiological validation for his core distinction. The "acquisition" system maps onto procedural memory; the "learning" system maps onto declarative memory.

Even researchers who hold a "weak interface" position — such as Rod Ellis (2005, 2006) and Nick Ellis (2005, 2011), who argue that explicit knowledge can facilitate acquisition by directing attention — do not claim that grammar instruction directly builds communicative competence. No current mainstream SLA researcher holds that traditional grammar-translation instruction directly builds communicative competence.

The meta-analyses are misunderstood: measurement bias hides the truth

The most commonly cited defense of explicit grammar instruction comes from meta-analyses, particularly Norris and Ortega (2000, Language Learning), who synthesized 49 studies and found large effects for explicit instruction. However, the authors themselves acknowledged a critical limitation: the vast majority of outcome measures assessed explicit knowledge on constrained grammar tests, not communicative ability.

74% of studies in Norris & Ortega (2000) lasted two weeks or less — designs that inherently favour explicit instruction
g = 1.76 effect size for implicit instruction on delayed posttests (Kang, Sok & Han, 2019) — significantly larger than explicit instruction

A critical updated meta-analysis by Kang, Sok, and Han (2019, Language Teaching Research) — covering 54 studies spanning 1980–2015 — found that while explicit instruction had a minor advantage on immediate posttests, implicit instruction showed significantly longer-lasting effects on delayed posttests. This finding directly undercuts the narrative that explicit instruction is "more effective."

Taylor (2023, MEXTESOL Journal) articulated the core logical problem: "The finding that explicit instruction improved explicit knowledge does nothing to discredit the hypothesis that acquisition, and not explicit learning, is the source of our communicative ability." Teaching grammar and then testing grammar creates a tautology, not evidence of communicative development.

Error correction raises anxiety and inhibits acquisition

John Truscott argued in a series of influential papers (1996, 1999, 2007) that grammar correction in L2 writing is not just ineffective but actively harmful. His 2007 meta-analytic synthesis found an overall effect size of −0.155 — a small but negative effect, meaning correction slightly harmed writing accuracy. He identified a particularly insidious mechanism: apparent improvements in accuracy after correction may reflect avoidance — students simplifying their language to evade errors rather than developing more complex competence.

Horwitz, Horwitz, and Cope (1986, Modern Language Journal) established Foreign Language Anxiety (FLA) as a distinct phenomenon with three components: communication apprehension, test anxiety, and fear of negative evaluation. MacIntyre and Gardner (1994) demonstrated that anxiety adversely affects language processing at input, processing, and output stages.

Gregersen and Horwitz (2002) found that anxious learners believed the goal was "avoiding mistakes," while confident learners were "eager to talk without concern about making errors." Steinberg and Horwitz (1986) showed anxious learners avoided using L2 in novel or creative ways.

Krashen's Affective Filter Hypothesis (1985) synthesises these findings: negative emotions create a psychological barrier that blocks comprehensible input from reaching the acquisition system. Grammar-focused classrooms — with their emphasis on rules, accuracy, testing, and correction — systematically elevate the affective filter. As Krashen stated: "For language acquisition to really succeed, anxiety should be zero."

Fossilization: grammar instruction is part of the problem, not the solution

Selinker (1972, IRAL) coined "fossilization" to describe persistent L2 errors that resist instruction and correction. He estimated 95% of L2 learners fail to reach native-like competence. Critically, Selinker identified "transfer of training" — the instructional patterns themselves — as one of five processes causing fossilization.

Han (2004, 2012) proposed the Selective Fossilization Hypothesis, showing that fossilized forms tend to be communicatively redundant — they don't impede understanding. Grammar instruction has not been shown to prevent fossilization; in fact, the declarative memory pathway it creates may contribute to it by preventing the development of robust procedural representations.

Longitudinal classroom evidence: the Dutch studies

The most rigorous classroom evidence comes from longitudinal studies in Dutch secondary schools by Rousse-Malpat and colleagues (2019, 2022, 2024). Comparing Structure-Based (SB) methods (explicit grammar, rules, drills) to Dynamic Usage-Based (DUB) methods (implicit, high exposure, meaningful interaction, no grammar rules) over six years of instruction:

After 3 years: DUB students were better at speaking and writing.

After 6 years: Equal on standardized tests; DUB better on complexity and fluency in writing.

"Explicit grammar is not needed to achieve accuracy."— Rousse-Malpat et al. (2022), given sufficient time and input

Implicitly taught learners took longer to reach accuracy parity (less accurate after year 1, equal by year 2, equal or better by year 6), but ultimately matched or exceeded explicitly taught learners on all measures — while developing superior fluency and complexity. This finding directly mirrors the neurolinguistic prediction: procedural learning is slower to emerge but produces more robust, automatic competence.

Part II

What the evidence says works

Comprehensible input is not a hypothesis — it is an established fact

Bill VanPatten, one of the most rigorous SLA researchers of the past three decades, has stated plainly: "The role of communicatively embedded and comprehensible input is not a hypothesis in L2 acquisition… it's a fact." Krashen's Input Hypothesis (1982, 1985) proposed that language is acquired "in only one way" — by understanding messages containing structures slightly beyond the learner's current level (i+1). His Natural Order Hypothesis demonstrated that grammatical structures are acquired in a predictable sequence regardless of instructional order, meaning grammar syllabi cannot override developmental processes.

VanPatten and Cadierno's (1993, Studies in Second Language Acquisition) foundational study showed that Processing Instruction — which manipulates input to push learners toward form-meaning connections — produced learners who performed as well as traditionally instructed students on production tasks and outperformed them on interpretation tasks. Dozens of subsequent studies across multiple languages (Spanish, French, Italian, Japanese, Russian, German) have confirmed: PI equals or outperforms traditional instruction on production while consistently outperforming on comprehension.

From comprehensible to compelling: Krashen's refinement

Krashen's most important theoretical update came in 2011 with the Compelling Input Hypothesis: input must be not just comprehensible but so interesting that the learner "forgets that it is in another language." Compelling input induces what Csikszentmihalyi (1990) called "flow" — total absorption where self-consciousness and awareness of time disappear.

Krashen argued that compelling input "appears to eliminate the need for motivation, a conscious desire to improve" — further refined with Mason (2020) as the Optimal Input Hypothesis: optimal input is comprehensible, compelling, rich in structure, and abundant.

The practical implication is profound: the teacher's primary job is not to explain grammar but to provide input that students find genuinely interesting and just comprehensible enough. Self-selected reading consistently outperforms assigned reading (Lee, 2007), and student choice — a core Montessori principle — is central to acquisition.

TPRS: the most studied acquisition-based classroom method

Teaching Proficiency through Reading and Storytelling (TPRS), developed by Spanish teacher Blaine Ray in the late 1980s, is the most extensively studied CI-based classroom method. It operates through three phases: establishing meaning, co-creating a story through repetitive "circling" questions, and reading.

Dr. Karen Lichtman (Binghamton University), the leading TPRS researcher, systematically reviewed 30 comparative studies and found:

21 / 30 studies showed advantages for TPRS
0 / 30 studies showed traditional methods outperforming TPRS

Key individual studies include:

Beal (2011, University of Kansas) — the largest TPRS study: 817 students across multiple districts. TPRS significantly outperformed traditional instruction in middle school, with lower anxiety and higher continued enrollment. Pippins and Krashen (2016) — 13 TPRS-prepared high school students averaged 3.5 on the AP Spanish exam, exactly matching the national average despite no traditional grammar preparation. Mason (Japan, 2020–2023) — 112 students over 3 years; despite control groups spending 286 extra hours on homework, memorisation, and grammar drills, there was no significant difference in final exam scores.

d = 1.131 Overall effect size in a meta-analysis of TPR-based studies (ERIC, 2021), classified as a "strong" effect (p < .01). Beyond test scores, TPRS consistently produces motivational benefits — students find it more enjoyable, less anxiety-provoking, and more confidence-building.

Extensive reading: doubling the rate of acquisition

The evidence for extensive reading / Free Voluntary Reading (FVR) as a language acquisition tool is among the strongest in the field. The landmark Fiji Book Flood study (Elley & Mangubhai, 1983, Reading Research Quarterly) provided 380 Grade 4–5 students with 250 high-interest story books in English.

2× After 8 months, Book Flood students progressed at twice the normal rate in reading and listening comprehension. By year 2, gains extended to grammar and writing — without any grammar instruction.

Elley (2000) replicated these findings across Niue, Singapore, Sri Lanka, South Africa, and the Solomon Islands. Krashen's synthesis of FVR research shows that in 38 out of 41 comparisons (93%), free readers performed as well as or better than students receiving direct instruction. In studies lasting one year or longer, 8 out of 9 comparisons showed free readers significantly outperforming instructed students.

Stokes, Krashen, and Kartchner (1998) demonstrated that reading even contributed to acquisition of the Spanish subjunctive — one of the most notoriously difficult grammar structures. The implications for a school library and classroom reading programme are clear and actionable.

The scale evidence: immersion and dual language programs

The strongest causal evidence for input-rich language education comes from large-scale programme evaluations. The RAND/Portland study (Steele et al., 2017, American Educational Research Journal) used enrollment lottery randomisation — the gold standard for causal inference — with 1,625 students across 12 dual language immersion (DLI) schools.

+22 percentile points on state reading tests by grade 8 for students randomly assigned to dual language immersion
2–4% additional per-pupil spending — the differential cost of DLI over standard instruction

Thomas and Collier's 32-year research programme (synthesised 2017, Annual Review of Applied Linguistics), analysing over 7.5 million student records across 36 districts in 16 U.S. states, found that high-quality dual language programmes are the only programme model that fully closes the achievement gap for English learners.

Canadian French immersion research spanning 60 years and over 300,000 students shows students developing high L2 proficiency at no cost to academic success, with cognitive benefits including heightened mental flexibility, creative thinking, and metalinguistic awareness (Lazaruk, 2007).

Part III

The Spanish and European context

Spain's proficiency problem reveals the failure of traditional methods

Spain's language education outcomes provide a natural case study in the consequences of grammar-focused instruction. On the 2025 EF English Proficiency Index, Spain ranks 36th globally with a score of 540 — classified as "Moderate" proficiency — trailing virtually all EU member states. The Netherlands (624), Croatia (617), Austria (616), and Germany (615) all score dramatically higher.

462 Spain's speaking score — the weakest of all four skills, and precisely the skill most dependent on procedural memory. Researchers identify traditional grammar-focused pedagogy as treating English "as if it were a theoretical subject rather than a practical one" (Llurda, 2024).

Eurostat data (2016) showed that 45.8% of Spaniards aged 25–64 could not speak any foreign language. Contributing factors include Spain's dubbing culture (versus subtitling in high-proficiency countries), Spanish as a powerful global language reducing perceived need, and traditional pedagogy.

CLIL in Spain: the right idea, problematic execution

Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL) has been Spain's dominant reform strategy since the mid-2000s. Madrid's Bilingual Programme launched in 2004–05 with 26 schools and by 2016–17 covered 45% of public primary and secondary schools. All 17 autonomous communities now have CLIL programmes.

Results are mixed. Pérez Cañado's (2018) study of 2,024 students across 53 schools found CLIL had a beneficial impact on English competence without harming L1 or content learning. However, Anghel, Cabrales, and Carro (2016) found negative effects on content test scores in Madrid, especially for children from less-educated families.

The fundamental problem: CLIL in Spain often means traditional teaching conducted in English by teachers with limited English proficiency. This is not true acquisition-based instruction — it provides input that may be neither sufficiently comprehensible nor compelling. Despite 20 years of CLIL investment, Spain's EF EPI ranking has barely improved.

European policy supports acquisition-based approaches

The CEFR Companion Volume (2020) formalized the Council of Europe's action-oriented approach, explicitly framing learners as "language users and social agents" and defining the goal as "learning to use the language rather than just learning about the language." The CEFR's "can-do" descriptors assess communicative competence profiles, not discrete-point grammar knowledge — an assessment philosophy fundamentally aligned with acquisition-based teaching.

Spain's LOMLOE (2020, implemented 2022/23) aligns with this framework, designating plurilingual competence as a key competence, organising the foreign language curriculum around Communication, Multilingualism, and Interculturality, and giving priority to oral comprehension, expression, and interaction in Primary Education.

The EU's Erasmus+ Bilingual Montessori Project (2023–2025), with partners in the Czech Republic, Estonia, Spain, and France, is currently developing resources specifically for Montessori bilingual education — a direct indicator of European-level institutional support for this approach.

Part IV

Why Montessori and acquisition-based methods are natural partners

Philosophical alignment runs deep

The alignment between Montessori principles and acquisition-based language teaching is not superficial — it reflects shared assumptions about how learning works. Maria Montessori's concept of the "absorbent mind" (1949) — the child's capacity to internalize language unconsciously from the environment — directly parallels Krashen's acquisition-learning distinction and the neurolinguistic evidence that procedural memory drives implicit language competence. Montessori observed that "learning a second language later in life is a conscious undertaking" — articulating the acquisition-learning distinction decades before Krashen formalized it.

The correspondences are systematic:

Prepared Environment → Prepared Linguistic Environment. Montessori classrooms are carefully designed with materials organized by complexity, supporting self-directed exploration. A CI classroom functions identically for language: rich input at varied levels (graded readers, audio, visual materials, compelling stories), organized to support self-selected engagement.

Following the Child → Learner-Centred Input. Montessori's principle of observing each child's readiness and interests maps directly onto providing comprehensible input at the learner's i+1 level and allowing self-selected reading. Krashen's compelling input hypothesis requires knowing and honoring what each child finds genuinely engaging.

Intrinsic Motivation / No Grades → Low Affective Filter. Montessori's rejection of external rewards and competitive grading creates precisely the low-anxiety conditions Krashen identifies as essential for acquisition. Self-paced work eliminates public failure. Mixed-age groupings reduce social comparison. Every feature of Montessori pedagogy works to lower the affective filter.

Cosmic Education → Content-Based Language Teaching. Montessori's integrated curriculum for ages 6–12, weaving language through science, history, geography, and culture via "Great Stories," directly parallels content-based instruction — one of the most effective vehicles for comprehensible input. Language learned through compelling content about the universe is both more motivating and more deeply processed.

Rosales Chavarria (2021, Journal of Montessori Research) documented efforts to align L2 teaching with Montessori pedagogy in a Mexican Children's House, transitioning toward immersion-based, learner-directed approaches. While rigorous comparative data from Montessori CI implementations remains a research gap, the theoretical alignment is strong enough that implementation represents a logical extension of existing Montessori practice rather than a departure from it.

Conclusion

The research presented here is not a collection of isolated findings but a convergent body of evidence from neurolinguistics, cognitive science, meta-analyses, longitudinal classroom studies, and large-scale programme evaluations — all pointing in the same direction. Explicit grammar instruction creates declarative memory representations that are neuroanatomically and computationally distinct from the procedural competence required for fluent speech.

Three insights deserve particular emphasis for decision-makers.

First, the "grammar first, fluency later" assumption has no neuroscientific basis. Paradis (2009), Ullman (2004, 2016), and Morgan-Short et al. (2012) have shown that declarative grammar knowledge does not convert into procedural competence through practice — it is replaced by separately acquired implicit knowledge through communicative experience. Every hour spent on grammar worksheets is an hour not spent providing the comprehensible input that actually builds fluency.

Second, the evidence for acquisition-based alternatives is substantial and growing. TPRS outperforms traditional methods in roughly 70% of comparative studies, with no studies showing traditional methods superior (Lichtman, 2019). The RAND/Portland lottery study (Steele et al., 2017) provides gold-standard causal evidence that input-rich education produces large, lasting gains. The Dutch longitudinal studies (Rousse-Malpat et al., 2019–2024) show that after six years, implicitly taught students match explicitly taught students on accuracy while exceeding them on fluency and complexity.

Third, Spain's persistent low English proficiency despite decades of grammar-focused instruction and CLIL investment is itself evidence that current approaches are failing. The EF EPI speaking score of 462 — the weakest skill — is precisely the outcome neurolinguistic research would predict from a system that channels language learning into declarative memory.

A Montessori school adopting CI-based language instruction is not experimenting with an untested approach. It is aligning its language programme with the same principles — the prepared environment, following the child, intrinsic motivation, learning through meaningful engagement rather than explicit instruction — that already define every other domain of Montessori education. The question is not whether the evidence supports this shift, but why any school committed to Montessori principles would maintain a grammar-translation approach that contradicts them.
