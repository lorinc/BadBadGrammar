# Why grammar textbooks fail and what neuroscience says works instead

**Traditional grammar-focused instruction routes language knowledge into the wrong brain system — one that cannot produce fluent speech.** Four decades of converging evidence from neurolinguistics, cognitive science, and classroom research demonstrate that explicit grammar teaching builds declarative memory representations (facts *about* language) rather than the procedural memory competence (automatic language *use*) that underlies all fluent communication. Meanwhile, acquisition-based methods grounded in comprehensible input consistently produce equal or superior outcomes in controlled studies, with particular advantages in fluency, long-term retention, and student motivation. This report synthesizes the foundational and recent evidence across both domains, with attention to the European and Spanish context and alignment with Montessori educational principles.

## Part I: The case against grammar-focused instruction

### Two brain systems, two kinds of knowledge — and only one produces speech

The strongest scientific argument against grammar instruction as a path to fluency comes from neurolinguistics. Michel Paradis (2004, 2009), drawing on 25+ years of bilingual aphasia data, neuroimaging, and neurological pathology, demonstrated that **implicit linguistic competence and explicit metalinguistic knowledge are stored in fundamentally different brain systems**. Implicit competence — the kind that enables fluent, automatic speech — relies on procedural memory, subserved by the basal ganglia, cerebellum, and left perisylvian cortex. Explicit grammar knowledge — the kind produced by classroom instruction — relies on declarative memory, subserved by the hippocampus and medial temporal lobes.

Paradis's critical conclusion is unequivocal: "Metalinguistic knowledge never becomes implicit competence, or the other way around" (Paradis, 2004, 2009). The two systems are doubly dissociated in brain pathology. Patients with amnesia (impaired declarative memory) retain all implicit linguistic competence. Patients with aphasia (impaired procedural language circuits) lose speech production but retain explicit grammar knowledge. Parkinson's disease patients show degraded procedural grammar; Alzheimer's patients lose declarative knowledge first — and notably, late-learned L2 has been reported to deteriorate early in Alzheimer's, suggesting it was primarily stored in declarative memory.

Michael Ullman's Declarative/Procedural (DP) Model (2001, 2004, 2016) extends this framework with detailed neurocognitive mapping. In native speakers, grammar processing depends on procedural memory circuits (Broca's area/BA 44, caudate, putamen). In L2 learners taught explicitly, grammar is initially processed through declarative memory — a fundamentally different computational substrate. A landmark neuroimaging meta-analysis by Tagarelli, Shattuck, Turkeltaub, and Ullman (2019, *NeuroImage*) confirmed this directly: **grammar learning with explicit training activated medial temporal lobe (declarative) regions, whereas grammar learning without explicit instruction activated basal ganglia (procedural) regions**. The type of instruction literally determined which brain system was recruited.

Can declarative grammar knowledge eventually "convert" to procedural competence? Hamrick, Lum, and Ullman (2018, *PNAS*) conducted multiple meta-analyses showing that L2 grammar was associated with only declarative memory at lower experience levels but with only procedural memory at higher experience levels. This shift happens — but through extensive communicative use, not through more grammar practice. Paradis (2009) clarifies that what occurs is not conversion but *substitution*: implicit competence is separately and incidentally acquired through communicative use, and its use gradually replaces reliance on explicit knowledge. Grammar instruction cannot accelerate this process because it feeds the wrong system.

### Morgan-Short's experiments: same test scores, radically different brains

The most direct experimental evidence comes from Kara Morgan-Short and colleagues (2012, *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*). Adults learned an artificial language under either **explicit training** (grammar rules explained, approximating classroom instruction) or **implicit training** (immersion-like, no rules given). At high proficiency, both groups performed identically on behavioral tests. But ERP brain recordings told a starkly different story.

**Only the implicitly trained group showed the full native-speaker brain signature** — an anterior negativity followed by a P600, the pattern characteristic of automatic syntactic processing. The explicitly trained group showed an anterior *positivity* followed by a P600 — a pattern reflecting reliance on domain-general attentional control mechanisms rather than native-like syntactic processing. As Morgan-Short et al. concluded: "Only implicit training led to an electrophysiological signature typical of native speakers." A follow-up retention study (Morgan-Short, Finger, Grey, & Ullman, 2012, *PLoS ONE*) found that after months of no exposure, the implicitly trained group *still* showed more native-like processing than the explicitly trained group. Training type has lasting neural consequences.

Complementary evidence from Bowden, Steinhauer, Sanz, and Ullman (2013, *Neuropsychologia*) showed that university L2 Spanish learners achieved native-like brain responses — but only those who had immersion experience beyond classroom instruction. The classroom-only group did not achieve native-like neural processing. Williams (2020, *Language Learning*) reviewed the neuroscience of implicit learning and confirmed a competitive relationship between declarative and procedural learning systems, consistent with the prediction that **explicit grammar instruction can actively inhibit procedural learning**.

For K-12 learners, this evidence is especially consequential. Neuroimaging meta-analyses of bilingual brain representation (reported in *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 2019) confirm that children and adolescents still have strong access to procedural memory for language learning. Pushing grammar instruction on school-age learners channels acquisition into the declarative system precisely when the procedural system is most available — wasting a developmental window that closes with age.

### Krashen's framework: theoretical foundations validated by neuroscience

Stephen Krashen's Monitor Model (1981, 1982, 1985) anticipated these neurolinguistic findings by two decades. His **Acquisition-Learning Distinction** proposed that "acquired" language (subconscious, meaning-focused) and "learned" language (conscious grammar rules) are fundamentally separate systems. His **Monitor Hypothesis** specified that learned knowledge can only function as a post-production editor, operating only when the speaker knows the rule, has time to apply it, and is focused on correctness. Krashen identified "Monitor over-users" — typically perfectionistic students produced by grammar-heavy classrooms — who "produce slowly, inadequately but correctly" because their learned rules interfere with fluent output.

While Krashen's theories were criticized for lack of falsifiability (McLaughlin, 1987; Gregg, 1984), the neurolinguistic evidence from Paradis, Ullman, and Morgan-Short now provides independent neurobiological validation for his core distinction. The "acquisition" system maps onto procedural memory; the "learning" system maps onto declarative memory. Paradis (2009) explicitly defends the no-interface view: "Given the premise that metalinguistic knowledge cannot be converted into implicit competence, there can be no possible interface between the two."

Even researchers who hold a "weak interface" position — such as Rod Ellis (2005, 2006) and Nick Ellis (2005, 2011), who argue that explicit knowledge can *facilitate* acquisition by directing attention — do not claim that grammar instruction directly builds communicative competence. The weak interface supports, at most, brief, well-timed focus on form within communicative activities. **No current mainstream SLA researcher holds that traditional grammar-translation instruction directly builds communicative competence.**

### The meta-analyses are misunderstood: measurement bias hides the truth

The most commonly cited defense of explicit grammar instruction comes from meta-analyses, particularly Norris and Ortega (2000, *Language Learning*), who synthesized 49 studies and found large effects for explicit instruction. However, the authors themselves acknowledged a critical limitation: **the vast majority of outcome measures assessed explicit knowledge on constrained grammar tests, not communicative ability**. Spada (2014), co-author of the follow-up Spada and Tomita (2010) meta-analysis, acknowledged the same: "The tests represented in the examined experiments assessed explicit knowledge rather than the implicit knowledge that is generally believed to be responsible for communication."

Most studies were also extremely brief: **21% were completed in a single day and 74% lasted two weeks or less** (Norris & Ortega, 2000). These designs inherently favor explicit instruction, which produces faster gains on controlled tests but not necessarily lasting communicative ability. A critical updated meta-analysis by **Kang, Sok, and Han (2019, *Language Teaching Research*)** — covering 54 studies spanning 1980–2015 — found that while explicit instruction had a minor advantage on immediate posttests, **implicit instruction showed significantly longer-lasting effects on delayed posttests** (implicit: g = 1.76). This finding directly undercuts the narrative that explicit instruction is "more effective."

Taylor (2023, *MEXTESOL Journal*) articulated the core logical problem: "The finding that explicit instruction improved explicit knowledge does nothing to discredit the hypothesis that acquisition, and not explicit learning, is the source of our communicative ability." Teaching grammar and then testing grammar creates a tautology, not evidence of communicative development.

### Error correction raises anxiety and inhibits acquisition

John Truscott argued in a series of influential papers (1996, 1999, 2007) that grammar correction in L2 writing is not just ineffective but actively harmful. His 2007 meta-analytic synthesis found an overall effect size of **−0.155** — a small but negative effect, meaning correction slightly *harmed* writing accuracy. He identified a particularly insidious mechanism: apparent improvements in accuracy after correction may reflect **avoidance** — students simplifying their language to evade errors rather than developing more complex competence. Li (2010, *Language Learning*) confirmed in a broader corrective feedback meta-analysis that while explicit feedback produced larger immediate effects, **implicit feedback effects were better maintained over time**.

The anxiety pathway compounds the damage. Horwitz, Horwitz, and Cope (1986, *Modern Language Journal*) established Foreign Language Anxiety (FLA) as a distinct phenomenon with three components: communication apprehension, test anxiety, and fear of negative evaluation. MacIntyre and Gardner (1994, *Language Learning*) demonstrated that anxiety adversely affects language processing at input, processing, *and* output stages. Gregersen (2003) linked FLA directly to "stringent error correction used by teachers." Gregersen and Horwitz (2002) found that anxious learners believed the goal was "avoiding mistakes," while confident learners were "eager to talk without concern about making errors." Steinberg and Horwitz (1986) showed anxious learners avoided using L2 in novel or creative ways.

Krashen's **Affective Filter Hypothesis** (1985) synthesizes these findings: negative emotions create a psychological barrier that blocks comprehensible input from reaching the acquisition system. Grammar-focused classrooms — with their emphasis on rules, accuracy, testing, and correction — systematically elevate the affective filter. As Krashen stated: "For language acquisition to really succeed, anxiety should be zero."

### Fossilization: grammar instruction is part of the problem, not the solution

Selinker (1972, *IRAL*) coined "fossilization" to describe persistent L2 errors that resist instruction and correction. He estimated **95% of L2 learners fail to reach native-like competence**. Critically, Selinker identified **"transfer of training"** — the instructional patterns themselves — as one of five processes causing fossilization. Han (2004, 2012) proposed the Selective Fossilization Hypothesis, showing that fossilized forms tend to be communicatively redundant — they don't impede understanding. The "Although...but..." construction (Han & Lew, 2012) is a classic example. Grammar instruction has not been shown to prevent fossilization; in fact, the declarative memory pathway it creates may contribute to it by preventing the development of robust procedural representations.

### Longitudinal classroom evidence: the Dutch studies

The most rigorous classroom evidence comes from longitudinal studies in Dutch secondary schools by Rousse-Malpat and colleagues (2019, 2022, 2024). Comparing Structure-Based (SB) methods (explicit grammar, rules, drills) to Dynamic Usage-Based (DUB) methods (implicit, high exposure, meaningful interaction, no grammar rules) over **six years** of instruction:

- After 3 years: DUB students were better at speaking and writing
- After 6 years: No differences on standardized reading/listening exams; DUB students performed **equally well on accuracy** but **better on complexity and fluency** in writing
- Key conclusion: **"Explicit grammar is not needed to achieve accuracy"** given sufficient time and input

Implicitly taught learners took longer to reach accuracy parity (less accurate after year 1, equal by year 2, equal or better by year 6), but ultimately matched or exceeded explicitly taught learners on all measures — while developing superior fluency and complexity. This finding directly mirrors the neurolinguistic prediction: procedural learning is slower to emerge but produces more robust, automatic competence.

## Part II: What the evidence says works

### Comprehensible input is not a hypothesis — it is an established fact

Bill VanPatten, one of the most rigorous SLA researchers of the past three decades, has stated plainly: "The role of communicatively embedded and comprehensible input is not a hypothesis in L2 acquisition… it's a fact." Krashen's **Input Hypothesis** (1982, 1985) proposed that language is acquired "in only one way" — by understanding messages containing structures slightly beyond the learner's current level (i+1). His **Natural Order Hypothesis** demonstrated that grammatical structures are acquired in a predictable sequence regardless of instructional order, meaning grammar syllabi cannot override developmental processes.

The evidence base supporting input as the primary driver of acquisition is now vast. VanPatten and Cadierno's (1993, *Studies in Second Language Acquisition*) foundational study showed that **Processing Instruction** — which manipulates input to push learners toward form-meaning connections — produced learners who performed as well as traditionally instructed students on production tasks *and* outperformed them on interpretation tasks. Dozens of subsequent studies across multiple languages (Spanish, French, Italian, Japanese, Russian, German) have confirmed: PI equals or outperforms traditional instruction on production while consistently outperforming on comprehension (Wong, 2004; Benati, 2004; Farley, 2001, 2004; Sanz, 2004).

VanPatten's Input Processing theory identifies specific principles governing how learners process input, including the **Primacy of Meaning Principle** (learners process input for meaning before form) and the **First Noun Principle** (learners tend to assign agent role to the first noun encountered). These principles explain *why* grammar instruction often fails: it asks learners to focus on form when their processing system prioritizes meaning. Effective instruction works *with* these processing tendencies rather than against them.

### From comprehensible to compelling: Krashen's refinement

Krashen's most important theoretical update came in 2011 with the **Compelling Input Hypothesis**: input must be not just comprehensible but so interesting that the learner "forgets that it is in another language." Compelling input induces what Csikszentmihalyi (1990) called "flow" — total absorption where self-consciousness and awareness of time disappear. Krashen argued that compelling input "appears to eliminate the need for motivation, a conscious desire to improve" (Krashen, 2011, *The English Connection*). This was further refined in Krashen and Mason (2020) as the **Optimal Input Hypothesis**: optimal input is comprehensible, compelling, rich in structure, and abundant.

The practical implication is profound: the teacher's primary job is not to explain grammar but to provide input that students find genuinely interesting and just comprehensible enough. **Self-selected reading consistently outperforms assigned reading** (Lee, 2007), and student choice — a core Montessori principle — is central to acquisition.

### TPRS: the most studied acquisition-based classroom method

Teaching Proficiency through Reading and Storytelling (TPRS), developed by Spanish teacher Blaine Ray in the late 1980s, is the most extensively studied CI-based classroom method. It operates through three phases: establishing meaning (introducing 3 target structures with gestures and L1 translation), co-creating a story through "circling" questions (repetitive yes/no, either/or, and open-ended questions that provide massive repetition of target structures), and reading (processing written input through class readers and embedded readings).

Dr. Karen Lichtman (Binghamton University), the leading TPRS researcher, systematically reviewed **30 comparative studies** and found that **21 showed advantages for TPRS, 7 showed mixed results, 2 showed no difference, and none showed traditional methods outperforming TPRS** (Lichtman, 2019). Her summary: "TPRS shows the clearest advantage for the acquisition of vocabulary and grammar, as well as the development of reading and speaking skills. Additionally, TPRS outperforms other methods on rate of learning."

Key individual studies include:

- **Watson (2009, *IJFLT*):** High school Spanish; TPRS students significantly outperformed traditional students in reading, listening, and speaking after one year
- **Spangler (2009, Walden University):** 162 middle/high school Spanish students over 14 weeks; TPRS outperformed Communicative Language Teaching on STAMP 4S assessment, with lower anxiety and higher motivation
- **Dziedzic (2012, *IJFLT*):** High school Spanish over 1 year; CI-based classes outperformed traditional in writing and speaking
- **Beal (2011, University of Kansas):** The largest TPRS study — **817 students** across multiple districts; TPRS significantly outperformed traditional instruction in middle school, with lower anxiety and higher continued enrollment
- **Pippins and Krashen (2016, *IJFLT*):** 13 TPRS-prepared high school students averaged **3.5 on the AP Spanish exam**, exactly matching the national average despite no traditional grammar preparation
- **Mason (Japan, 2020–2023):** 112 public junior high school students over 3 years; despite control groups spending **286 extra hours** on homework, memorization, and grammar drills, there was no significant difference in final exam scores — the CI group achieved the same results with dramatically less total study time

A meta-analysis of TPR-based studies (ERIC, 2021) found an overall effect size of **d = 1.131** (p < .01), classified as a "strong" effect. Beyond test scores, TPRS consistently produces motivational benefits: multiple studies report students finding TPRS classes more enjoyable (Arnoldus 2009: 68% preferred TPRS), less anxiety-provoking (Spangler, 2009; Beal, 2011), and more confidence-building (Murray, 2014; Dukes, 2012).

### Extensive reading: doubling the rate of acquisition

The evidence for extensive reading / Free Voluntary Reading (FVR) as a language acquisition tool is among the strongest in the field. The landmark **Fiji Book Flood study** (Elley & Mangubhai, 1983, *Reading Research Quarterly*) provided 380 Grade 4–5 students with 250 high-interest story books in English. After 8 months, **Book Flood students progressed at twice the normal rate** in reading and listening comprehension. By year 2, gains extended to grammar and writing — without any grammar instruction. Elley (2000) replicated these findings across Niue, Singapore, Sri Lanka, South Africa, and the Solomon Islands, concluding it is possible to "double the rate of reading acquisition" with approximately 100 high-interest books per class.

Krashen's synthesis of FVR research shows that in **38 out of 41 comparisons (93%)**, free readers performed as well as or better than students receiving direct instruction. In studies lasting one year or longer, **8 out of 9 comparisons** showed free readers significantly outperforming instructed students (Krashen, 2004, 2011). Stokes, Krashen, and Kartchner (1998) demonstrated that reading even contributed to acquisition of the Spanish subjunctive — one of the most notoriously difficult grammar structures. The implications for a school library and classroom reading program are clear and actionable.

### The scale evidence: immersion and dual language programs

The strongest causal evidence for input-rich language education comes from large-scale program evaluations. The **RAND/Portland study** (Steele et al., 2017, *American Educational Research Journal*) used enrollment lottery randomization — the gold standard for causal inference — with **1,625 students** across 12 dual language immersion (DLI) schools. Students randomly assigned to DLI **outperformed peers on state reading tests by 13 percentile points in grade 5 and 22 percentile points in grade 8**, with no detriment in math or science. Eighth-graders reached Intermediate Mid proficiency versus Novice Mid for students taking traditional language electives. Differential costs were modest: **2–4% of per-pupil spending**.

Thomas and Collier's 32-year research program (synthesized 2017, *Annual Review of Applied Linguistics*), analyzing **over 7.5 million student records across 36 districts in 16 U.S. states**, found that high-quality dual language programs are the *only* program model that fully closes the achievement gap for English learners. A 2025 Georgia lottery study (Morales, *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*) confirmed DLI benefits: reading gains of +0.12 SD and math gains of +0.14 SD, realized as early as first grade.

Canadian French immersion research spanning 60 years and **over 300,000 students** shows students developing high L2 proficiency at no cost to academic success, with cognitive benefits including heightened mental flexibility, creative thinking, and metalinguistic awareness (Lazaruk, 2007, *Canadian Modern Language Review*).

## Part III: The Spanish and European context

### Spain's proficiency problem reveals the failure of traditional methods

Spain's language education outcomes provide a natural case study in the consequences of grammar-focused instruction. On the **2025 EF English Proficiency Index, Spain ranks 36th globally with a score of 540** — classified as "Moderate" proficiency — trailing virtually all EU member states. The Netherlands (624), Croatia (617), Austria (616), and Germany (615) all score dramatically higher. Spain's weakest skill is **speaking (462)**, precisely the skill most dependent on procedural memory and most damaged by grammar-focused instruction.

Eurostat data (2016) showed that **45.8% of Spaniards aged 25–64 could not speak any foreign language**. A 2017 CIS poll found nearly 60% of Spanish respondents reported they could not read, write, or speak English. Researchers identify contributing factors: Spain's dubbing culture (versus subtitling in high-proficiency countries), Spanish as a powerful global language reducing perceived need, and — critically — **traditional grammar-focused pedagogy that treats English "as if it were a theoretical subject rather than a practical one"** (Llurda, 2024, *World Englishes*).

### CLIL in Spain: the right idea, problematic execution

Content and Language Integrated Learning (CLIL) has been Spain's dominant reform strategy since the mid-2000s. Madrid's Bilingual Programme launched in 2004–05 with 26 schools and by 2016–17 covered **45% of public primary and secondary schools**. All 17 autonomous communities now have CLIL programs.

Results are mixed. Pérez Cañado's (2018) study of **2,024 students across 53 schools in 12 Spanish provinces** — the largest controlled study — found CLIL had a beneficial impact on English competence without harming L1 or content learning. However, Anghel, Cabrales, and Carro (2016) found **negative effects** on content test scores in Madrid, especially for children from less-educated families. Bruton (2011, 2013) argued CLIL research contains "numerous anomalies" and that benefits may reflect selection effects rather than method effectiveness.

The fundamental problem: CLIL in Spain often means **traditional teaching conducted in English by teachers with limited English proficiency** (identified as a consistent concern in a Cambridge Core 2024 review). This is not true acquisition-based instruction — it provides input that may be neither sufficiently comprehensible nor compelling. El País reported in 2023 that "school bilingualism fails in its objective of generalizing the learning of English." Despite 20 years of CLIL investment, Spain's EF EPI ranking has barely improved.

### European policy supports acquisition-based approaches

The **CEFR Companion Volume (2020)** formalized the Council of Europe's action-oriented approach, explicitly framing learners as "language users and social agents" and defining the goal as "learning to use the language rather than just learning about the language." The CEFR's "can-do" descriptors assess communicative competence profiles, not discrete-point grammar knowledge — an assessment philosophy fundamentally aligned with acquisition-based teaching.

Spain's **LOMLOE** (2020, implemented 2022/23) aligns with this framework, designating plurilingual competence as a key competence, organizing the foreign language curriculum around Communication, Multilingualism, and Interculturality, and giving priority to oral comprehension, expression, and interaction in Primary Education. The policy environment is receptive to acquisition-based methods; the gap is in implementation.

The **2002 Barcelona Objective** — that every EU citizen should speak two languages beyond their mother tongue — and the 2018 Council Recommendation on language teaching both support the kind of systematic, input-rich language education that acquisition-based methods provide. The EU's **Erasmus+ Bilingual Montessori Project** (2023–2025), with partners in the Czech Republic, Estonia, Spain, and France, is currently developing resources specifically for Montessori bilingual education — a direct indicator of European-level institutional support.

## Part IV: Why Montessori and acquisition-based methods are natural partners

### Philosophical alignment runs deep

The alignment between Montessori principles and acquisition-based language teaching is not superficial — it reflects shared assumptions about how learning works. Maria Montessori's concept of the **"absorbent mind"** (1949) — the child's capacity to internalize language unconsciously from the environment — directly parallels Krashen's acquisition-learning distinction and the neurolinguistic evidence that procedural memory drives implicit language competence. Montessori observed that "learning a second language later in life is a conscious undertaking" — articulating the acquisition-learning distinction decades before Krashen formalized it.

The correspondences are systematic:

**Prepared Environment → Prepared Linguistic Environment.** Montessori classrooms are carefully designed with materials organized by complexity, supporting self-directed exploration. A CI classroom functions identically for language: rich input at varied levels (graded readers, audio, visual materials, compelling stories), organized to support self-selected engagement. The environment itself teaches.

**Following the Child → Learner-Centered Input.** Montessori's principle of observing each child's readiness and interests maps directly onto providing comprehensible input at the learner's i+1 level and allowing self-selected reading. Krashen's compelling input hypothesis — that input must be so interesting the learner forgets it's in another language — requires knowing and honoring what each child finds genuinely engaging.

**Intrinsic Motivation / No Grades → Low Affective Filter.** Montessori's rejection of external rewards and competitive grading creates precisely the low-anxiety conditions Krashen identifies as essential for acquisition. Self-paced work eliminates public failure. Mixed-age groupings reduce social comparison. The teacher as "guide" rather than judge eliminates fear of evaluation. Every feature of Montessori pedagogy works to lower the affective filter.

**Auto-education → Implicit Acquisition.** Montessori's belief that children construct knowledge through interaction with their environment — not through direct instruction — parallels the SLA position that grammar is acquired implicitly through processing input, not learned through explicit explanation.

**Cosmic Education → Content-Based Language Teaching.** Montessori's integrated curriculum for ages 6–12, weaving language through science, history, geography, and culture via "Great Stories," directly parallels content-based instruction — one of the most effective vehicles for comprehensible input. Language learned through compelling content about the universe is both more motivating and more deeply processed.

Rosales Chavarria (2021, *Journal of Montessori Research*) documented efforts to align L2 teaching with Montessori pedagogy in a Mexican Children's House, transitioning toward immersion-based, learner-directed approaches. Winnefeld (2012) studied task-based language learning in bilingual Montessori elementary schools in Germany. While rigorous comparative data from Montessori CI implementations remains a research gap, the theoretical alignment is strong enough that implementation represents a logical extension of existing Montessori practice rather than a departure from it.

## Conclusion: the evidence converges on a clear path forward

The research presented here is not a collection of isolated findings but a convergent body of evidence from neurolinguistics, cognitive science, meta-analyses, longitudinal classroom studies, and large-scale program evaluations — all pointing in the same direction. Explicit grammar instruction creates declarative memory representations that are neuroanatomically and computationally distinct from the procedural competence required for fluent speech. The meta-analyses most commonly cited to defend grammar teaching suffer from measurement bias — testing explicit knowledge with explicit tests. When studies use communicative measures and longer timelines, the advantage for explicit instruction disappears or reverses.

Three insights deserve particular emphasis for decision-makers. First, **the "grammar first, fluency later" assumption has no neuroscientific basis**. Paradis (2009), Ullman (2004, 2016), and Morgan-Short et al. (2012) have shown that declarative grammar knowledge does not convert into procedural competence through practice — it is replaced by separately acquired implicit knowledge through communicative experience. Every hour spent on grammar worksheets is an hour not spent providing the comprehensible input that actually builds fluency.

Second, **the evidence for acquisition-based alternatives is substantial and growing**. TPRS outperforms traditional methods in roughly 70% of comparative studies, with no studies showing traditional methods superior (Lichtman, 2019). The RAND/Portland lottery study (Steele et al., 2017) provides gold-standard causal evidence that input-rich education produces large, lasting gains. The Dutch longitudinal studies (Rousse-Malpat et al., 2019–2024) show that after six years, implicitly taught students match explicitly taught students on accuracy while exceeding them on fluency and complexity.

Third, **Spain's persistent low English proficiency despite decades of grammar-focused instruction and CLIL investment is itself evidence** that current approaches are failing. The EF EPI speaking score of 462 — the weakest skill — is precisely the outcome neurolinguistic research would predict from a system that channels language learning into declarative memory. The CEFR, LOMLOE, and European policy frameworks already support the shift to acquisition-based methods; the Erasmus+ Bilingual Montessori Project signals institutional readiness.

A Montessori school adopting CI-based language instruction is not experimenting with an untested approach. It is aligning its language program with the same principles — the prepared environment, following the child, intrinsic motivation, learning through meaningful engagement rather than explicit instruction — that already define every other domain of Montessori education. The question is not whether the evidence supports this shift, but why any school committed to Montessori principles would maintain a grammar-translation approach that contradicts them.
