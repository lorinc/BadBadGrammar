# Rethinking How We Teach Languages

**What 40 years of brain science and classroom research tell us about language learning — and why it matters for our school**

*A research summary and practical guide for educators and school leadership*

---

## At a Glance

**The core finding:** When we teach a language through grammar rules and vocabulary lists, the brain stores that knowledge in a completely different system than the one it uses for actual speaking. It's like learning the rules of swimming from a textbook but never getting in the pool — except worse, because the "textbook knowledge" can actually get in the way when you finally do jump in.

**The problem isn't just inefficiency — it's misdirection.** Brain imaging studies show that students taught grammar rules process language through their *declarative memory* (the system for facts and dates), while fluent speakers use *procedural memory* (the system for riding a bike or playing piano). These are physically different brain circuits. And the research is clear: one does not become the other through practice. A student who memorises verb conjugation tables is not on the path to fluent speech — they're on a different path entirely.

**What does work?** The same thing that works for every child learning their first language: lots of meaningful, understandable input. Stories. Conversations. Reading. When students absorb language through compelling content they actually care about, their brains build the right neural pathways automatically — the ones that enable real-time, fluent communication.

**This isn't a fringe theory.** It's supported by neurolinguistic research (Paradis, 2009; Ullman, 2016), brain imaging experiments (Morgan-Short et al., 2012), six-year longitudinal studies in European schools (Rousse-Malpat et al., 2024), gold-standard randomised trials of immersion programs (Steele et al., 2017), and 30+ comparative studies of story-based teaching methods (Lichtman, 2019). The convergence across disciplines is remarkable.

**For a Montessori school, this is especially relevant.** Acquisition-based language teaching aligns perfectly with Montessori principles: the prepared environment, following the child, intrinsic motivation, learning through engagement rather than instruction. Adopting these methods isn't adding something foreign to our pedagogy — it's removing the one area where we're contradicting it.

> **This document covers three things:** (1) the evidence that traditional grammar instruction doesn't just underperform but actively builds the wrong brain pathways; (2) the evidence for what works instead, with real classroom results; and (3) a practical guide for how schools have successfully made the transition.

# Part 1: Why Grammar-First Teaching Backfires

## Your brain has two filing systems for language

Here's something most of us never learned in teacher training: the brain doesn't have one language system. It has two, and they work very differently.

**Procedural memory** is the system that handles skills you do automatically — riding a bike, tying your shoes, playing a practiced piece of music. For native speakers, grammar lives here. You don't think about subject-verb agreement when you speak your first language; you just *know* what sounds right. This system lives in brain structures called the basal ganglia and parts of the left hemisphere.

**Declarative memory** is the system for facts and conscious knowledge — the capital of France, the year you graduated, the rule for when to use the subjunctive. It's centred in the hippocampus and medial temporal lobe.

When we teach grammar rules, the brain files that knowledge in the declarative system. When we acquire language naturally — by understanding messages in context — it goes into the procedural system. This isn't a theoretical distinction. Neurolinguist Michel Paradis (2004, 2009) demonstrated it through decades of studying bilingual patients with brain injuries:

> Patients with damage to declarative memory (amnesia) kept their ability to speak fluently. Patients with damage to procedural circuits (aphasia) lost their speech but could still recite grammar rules. The two systems are physically separate.

Michael Ullman's Declarative/Procedural Model (2001, 2016) mapped this in detail, and a 2019 neuroimaging meta-analysis by Tagarelli and colleagues confirmed it directly: when people learn grammar through rules, their medial temporal lobe lights up (declarative memory); when they pick it up through immersion-like exposure, the basal ganglia activate (procedural memory). **The type of teaching literally determines which brain system gets recruited.**

## Can "learned" become "acquired"?

This is what everyone asks: "Okay, but surely with enough practice, the grammar rules eventually become automatic?"

The neuroscience says: not in the way you'd hope. What happens isn't that declarative knowledge "converts" into procedural skill. Instead, through lots of communicative experience, the brain separately and gradually builds procedural competence alongside the existing declarative knowledge. As Paradis puts it, implicit competence is "separately and incidentally acquired through communicative use" and its use eventually replaces reliance on rules.

Hamrick, Lum, and Ullman (2018), in *PNAS*, confirmed this: at lower levels, second-language grammar was associated only with declarative memory; at higher levels, only with procedural memory. But the shift happened through accumulated communicative experience — not through grammar drills. **Grammar instruction feeds the wrong system. More of it doesn't fix the problem; it deepens it.**

## Same test scores, radically different brains

The most striking evidence comes from a 2012 experiment by Kara Morgan-Short and colleagues at Georgetown, published in the *Journal of Cognitive Neuroscience*.

Two groups of adults learned an artificial language. One group got explicit grammar rules (like a traditional classroom). The other got immersion-like exposure (no rules, just meaningful practice). After enough training, **both groups scored equally well on grammar tests.** If you only looked at the test scores, you'd conclude both methods work.

But when researchers recorded their brain activity, the picture was completely different. Only the immersion group showed the brain signature of native-like language processing — the specific neural pattern that indicates automatic, fluent grammatical processing. The grammar-rules group showed a different pattern entirely, reflecting effortful, controlled processing — the brain working harder to achieve the same result.

Even more telling: in a follow-up months later, with no practice in between, the immersion group *still* showed more native-like processing. The grammar group hadn't caught up. The training method had created lasting differences in how the brain handled language.

## The anxiety trap

Grammar-focused teaching doesn't just route knowledge to the wrong brain system. It also creates emotional conditions that actively block acquisition.

Horwitz, Horwitz, and Cope (1986) identified Foreign Language Anxiety as a distinct phenomenon with three components: fear of speaking, test anxiety, and fear of being corrected. MacIntyre and Gardner (1994) showed this anxiety disrupts language processing at every stage — input, processing, and output. Gregersen and Horwitz (2002) found that anxious students believed the goal of language class was "avoiding mistakes," while confident students were "eager to talk without concern about making errors."

Grammar classrooms — with their emphasis on correctness, red-pen corrections, and getting-the-rule-right — systematically produce the first kind of student. Krashen calls this the **Affective Filter**: when anxiety goes up, the brain's ability to absorb new language goes down, regardless of how good the input is.

John Truscott (1996, 2007) took this further. His meta-analysis found a small *negative* effect (d = –0.155) for grammar correction in writing: corrected students performed marginally worse than uncorrected ones. He identified the mechanism: students don't learn from corrections — they learn to avoid complexity. They write simpler sentences to dodge errors, which looks like improvement but is actually a retreat from growth.

## The measurement illusion

At this point, sceptics often point to meta-analyses that seem to show grammar instruction "works." The most cited is Norris and Ortega (2000), which found large effects for explicit instruction across 49 studies.

But the authors themselves flagged a critical problem: nearly all the studies measured success with grammar tests. Teaching grammar and then testing grammar proves only that students can do grammar exercises — not that they can speak, understand, or communicate. As Taylor (2023) put it: "The finding that explicit instruction improved explicit knowledge does nothing to discredit the hypothesis that acquisition is the source of our communicative ability."

When researchers used communicative measures and longer timelines, the picture reversed. Kang, Sok, and Han (2019) analysed 54 studies spanning 35 years and found that while explicit instruction had a slight edge on immediate tests, **implicit instruction showed significantly stronger effects on delayed posttests** — in other words, on the knowledge that actually sticks.

## The six-year Dutch study: what happens with time

Most classroom studies run for weeks. Rousse-Malpat and colleagues in the Netherlands ran theirs for six years. They compared two approaches to teaching French in Dutch secondary schools:

- **Structure-Based:** explicit grammar rules, drills, textbook exercises
- **Dynamic Usage-Based:** implicit, high exposure, meaningful interaction, no grammar rules taught

Here's what they found (across papers in 2019, 2022, and 2024):

- **After 1 year:** Grammar-taught students were slightly more accurate (the rules work, short-term).
- **After 3 years:** Implicit-taught students were better at speaking and writing.
- **After 6 years:** No differences on standardised reading and listening exams. Writing accuracy was equal — but implicit-taught students had *better fluency and more complex output.*

The authors' conclusion: **"Explicit grammar is not needed to achieve accuracy"** — given enough time and enough input. The grammar approach offered a short-term illusion of progress. The acquisition approach produced the same accuracy *plus* fluency and complexity the other group never reached.

## The fossilisation problem

Larry Selinker coined the term "fossilisation" in 1972 to describe persistent errors that resist all instruction and correction. He estimated that **95% of second-language learners never reach native-like competence.** Why do some errors lock in permanently?

Selinker identified several causes, and one of them was striking: **"transfer of training"** — the instructional patterns themselves. In other words, *how you were taught* can become part of the fossilised pattern. Grammar instruction hasn't been shown to prevent fossilisation. The declarative pathway it builds may actually contribute to it, by giving students a way to produce "good enough" output without developing the procedural competence that would let them grow beyond it.

# Part 2: What Actually Works

## The central insight: comprehensible input

Here's the part that makes language teachers hopeful: we know what works, and it's not complicated.

Stephen Krashen's **Input Hypothesis** (1982, 1985) proposed that language is acquired "in only one way" — by understanding messages that are slightly beyond the learner's current level. Not below (too easy, no growth). Not far above (incomprehensible, no absorption). Just slightly beyond, so the brain stretches to make meaning.

This was controversial when proposed, but the evidence has piled up for four decades. As Bill VanPatten, one of the field's most rigorous researchers, has put it: "The role of communicatively embedded and comprehensible input is not a hypothesis in second language acquisition — it's a fact."

Krashen updated his thinking in 2011 with something even more useful for teachers: the **Compelling Input Hypothesis.** Input must be not just comprehensible but so interesting that the learner *forgets it's in another language.* Think about how kids get absorbed in a great story and don't notice an hour has passed — that's the state where acquisition happens most powerfully.

The practical implication is profound: our job isn't to explain grammar. It's to find input that students genuinely want to engage with, and make sure it's understandable. Everything else follows.

## TPRS: stories as curriculum

Teaching Proficiency through Reading and Storytelling (TPRS), developed by Spanish teacher Blaine Ray in the late 1980s, is the most extensively studied classroom method built on these principles.

The basic structure is simple:

1. **Establish meaning:** Introduce three target phrases with gestures and a quick translation.
2. **Co-create a story:** The teacher asks lots of repetitive questions ("Is there a boy? What's his name? Does he have a cat or a dog?") that generate the story collaboratively with students, providing massive repetition of the target phrases in context.
3. **Read:** Students then read a version of the story or related material, processing the same language in written form.

Karen Lichtman at Binghamton University, the leading TPRS researcher, systematically reviewed 30 comparative studies. The scorecard:

- **21 showed TPRS outperforming traditional methods**
- **7 showed mixed results**
- **2 showed no difference**
- **0 showed traditional methods winning**

Her summary: "TPRS shows the clearest advantage for the acquisition of vocabulary and grammar, as well as the development of reading and speaking skills. It also outperforms other methods on rate of learning."

Some specific studies worth knowing:

- **Beal (2011), University of Kansas** — the largest TPRS study, with 817 students across multiple US districts. TPRS significantly outperformed traditional instruction in middle school, with lower anxiety and higher rates of continued enrolment in language study.
- **Pippins and Krashen (2016)** — 13 TPRS-prepared high school students averaged 3.5 on the AP Spanish exam — exactly the US national average — despite having no traditional grammar preparation.
- **Mason (Japan, 2020–2023)** — 112 public junior high students over 3 years. Control groups spent 286 *additional* hours on homework, memorisation, and drills. Final exam scores were statistically identical. The TPRS group achieved the same results with dramatically less total study time.

A 2021 meta-analysis of TPR-based studies in ERIC found an overall effect size of d = 1.13 — classified as a "strong" effect in educational research.

Beyond test scores, TPRS consistently produces motivational benefits. Students report finding the classes enjoyable. Anxiety drops. Confidence rises. Arnoldus (2009) found 68% of students preferred TPRS to traditional teaching. These aren't small things — they're the conditions under which acquisition actually happens.

## Extensive reading: the most underused tool

If there's one intervention with an evidence base strong enough to call it a no-brainer, it's free voluntary reading.

The landmark study is the **Fiji Book Flood** (Elley and Mangubhai, 1983, *Reading Research Quarterly*). Researchers gave 380 grade 4–5 students 250 high-interest English storybooks. After 8 months, **Book Flood students progressed at twice the normal rate** in reading and listening comprehension. By year 2, gains extended to grammar and writing — with no grammar instruction at all. Elley replicated these findings across Niue, Singapore, Sri Lanka, South Africa, and the Solomon Islands. His conclusion: it's possible to "double the rate of reading acquisition" with about 100 high-interest books per class.

Krashen has synthesised this research across many contexts. In 38 out of 41 comparisons (93%), free readers performed as well as or better than students receiving direct instruction. In studies lasting a year or longer, 8 out of 9 comparisons showed free readers significantly outperforming instructed students.

Perhaps most striking: Stokes, Krashen, and Kartchner (1998) found that even the Spanish subjunctive — one of the most famously tricky grammar structures, the one textbooks spend entire chapters on — was acquired through reading alone, without explicit instruction.

The practical implication for any school with a budget: a well-stocked classroom library of level-appropriate, genuinely interesting books, with dedicated reading time, is probably the highest-leverage language investment you can make.

## The big-picture evidence: immersion at scale

The strongest evidence that input-rich education works at scale comes from program evaluations.

The **RAND/Portland study** (Steele et al., 2017, in the *American Educational Research Journal*) is the gold standard. It used enrolment lottery randomisation — students who applied to dual-language immersion schools were randomly assigned or not, giving researchers a genuinely controlled comparison across 1,625 students in 12 schools. Results:

- Students in immersion **outperformed peers on state reading tests by 13 percentile points in grade 5 and 22 percentile points in grade 8**
- No detriment in maths or science
- 8th-graders reached Intermediate Mid proficiency vs. Novice Mid for peers taking traditional language electives
- All this for just **2–4% more per-pupil spending**

Thomas and Collier's 32-year research program (synthesised 2017, *Annual Review of Applied Linguistics*), drawing on more than 7.5 million student records across 36 US districts, found that high-quality dual-language programs are the *only* program model that fully closes the academic achievement gap for English learners.

Canadian French immersion, running for 60+ years with over 300,000 students, shows consistent results: high second-language proficiency at no cost to academic performance, plus cognitive benefits including mental flexibility, creative thinking, and metalinguistic awareness (Lazaruk, 2007).

## A note on Spain's situation

Spain's language education outcomes illustrate what happens when a country invests heavily in grammar-focused teaching. On the 2025 EF English Proficiency Index, Spain ranks 36th globally — "Moderate" proficiency — trailing virtually every EU member state. The Netherlands, Croatia, Austria, and Germany all score dramatically higher. Spain's weakest skill is *speaking* (462) — precisely the skill most dependent on procedural memory and most damaged by grammar-focused instruction.

CLIL (Content and Language Integrated Learning) has been Spain's reform strategy since the mid-2000s and now covers roughly 45% of public schools. Results are mixed, and researchers have identified a consistent pattern: much "CLIL" is actually traditional teaching *conducted in English* by teachers with limited English themselves. It's not really immersion or acquisition-based teaching — it's the same methods in a different language. Unsurprisingly, outcomes have been modest.

The policy environment, however, is receptive. Spain's LOMLOE (2020) and the CEFR Companion Volume (2020) both explicitly frame learners as "language users" rather than "language students," with assessment built around what learners can *do* with the language. The framework supports acquisition-based methods; the bottleneck is implementation at the school level.

# Part 3: A Cookbook for Montessori Schools

## Why this fits our pedagogy naturally

Before we get to the practical steps, it's worth naming why this transition is less of a leap for a Montessori school than it would be for a conventional one. Maria Montessori wrote about the "absorbent mind" — the child's capacity to internalise language unconsciously from the environment — decades before Krashen's acquisition-learning distinction. She specifically observed that "learning a second language later in life is a conscious undertaking," distinguishing it from natural acquisition. She was describing what neuroscience has now confirmed.

The alignment is systematic:

| Montessori Principle | Acquisition-Based Teaching |
|---|---|
| Prepared environment | A room rich in target-language input: books, audio, visuals, stories |
| Following the child | Comprehensible input matched to each student's level; self-selected reading |
| Intrinsic motivation, no grades | Low affective filter — the precondition for acquisition |
| Auto-education | Implicit acquisition from meaningful engagement, not direct instruction |
| Mixed-age groupings | Older students provide natural, level-varied input for younger ones |
| Cosmic Education | Language taught through compelling content (science, history, culture) |

Adopting comprehensible-input methods isn't importing something foreign into our pedagogy. It's resolving the one place where current language teaching contradicts it.

## The transition, in phases

### Phase 1: The Prepared Linguistic Environment (Term 1)

**Build the library first.** Before changing any lesson plan, build a classroom library of 80–120 high-interest books in the target language, graded by level. Picture books for the youngest learners. Simple novellas for older students. A mix of fiction and non-fiction. The "Fiji Book Flood" research suggests ~100 books per class is the sweet spot.

Good sources: graded readers from publishers like Command Performance Language Institute, Fluency Matters, TPRS Books, and Wayside Publishing. For Spanish specifically, authors like Mira Canion, Carol Gaab, and Carrie Toth have written entire libraries of level-appropriate novels.

**Add audio and visuals.** Age-appropriate songs, podcasts, animated series with subtitles in the target language. The goal is making the target language an ambient presence, not just a scheduled subject.

### Phase 2: Teacher Development (Terms 1–2)

This is the non-negotiable. Teachers can't teach what they haven't experienced. Invest in:

- **TPRS training.** Organisations like Blaine Ray Workshops, Fluency Matters, and Express Fluency run intensive workshops. Many offer online options. Budget: roughly €400–800 per teacher for a quality multi-day workshop.
- **CI community membership.** Groups like the IFLT conference, Comprehensible Online, and Spanish/French-specific Facebook communities (e.g. "iFLT/NTPRS/CI Teaching") offer ongoing support.
- **Observations.** If possible, arrange for teachers to visit a school already using these methods. One day of watching is worth a month of reading about it.

Pair this with reading. Two practitioner books are essential: Karen Lichtman's *Teaching Proficiency through Reading and Storytelling (TPRS): An Input-Based Approach* (2018) and Stephen Krashen's *Principles and Practice in Second Language Acquisition* (free online at sdkrashen.com).

### Phase 3: Classroom Implementation (Terms 2–3)

A typical CI-based lesson has a rhythm:

- **5–10 minutes:** Warm-up in the target language — a personalised question the class discusses ("Who ate something unusual this weekend?"), heavily scaffolded with gestures, drawings, and occasional translation.
- **20–30 minutes:** Main input activity — a co-created story, a reading circle, a "Movie Talk" (narrating a short film clip in simple target language), or a discussion of a compelling image.
- **10–15 minutes:** Free reading from the classroom library. Students choose their own book. The only rule is that they read.
- **Optional closing:** A brief writing task — not a grammar exercise but a personal response ("Write three things you'd tell the character in our story").

What's notably *absent*: grammar explanations as a primary activity, vocabulary lists to memorise, fill-in-the-blank worksheets, verb conjugation tables. If a grammar point comes up naturally ("We keep hearing 'fue' in this story — it means 'he went'"), handle it in 30 seconds and move on. Never make it the lesson.

### Phase 4: Assessment Redesign (Term 3 onward)

This is where schools often stumble. If you change the teaching but keep the grammar tests, teachers will revert to grammar teaching — the tests will pull them back. New approaches to consider:

- **Proficiency-based assessments** aligned with the CEFR's "can-do" descriptors. ACTFL's AAPPL and STAMP tests offer external benchmarking.
- **Timed writes:** students write for 5–10 minutes on a prompt. Score for word count, comprehensibility, and complexity — not grammatical perfection.
- **Free retells:** students retell a story they've heard or read. Score for detail, fluency, and comprehensibility.
- **Extensive reading logs:** track pages read, with brief reflections. This honours the effort without testing it extractively.

Report card language also needs rethinking. Instead of "85% on grammar test," think "Can describe past events in 2–3 connected sentences" or "Reads independently at Intermediate-Low level." This is how the CEFR and ACTFL frameworks are built.

## Real schools that have done this

A few concrete examples of schools and programs that have made the transition, for board members who want proof of concept:

- **Denver Language School** (US) — dual-language immersion using CI methods, one of the highest-performing schools in Colorado across all subjects.
- **Uncommon Schools network** — adopted TPRS across their language programs; reported improved AP exam pass rates and dramatically higher student enrolment in upper-level language courses.
- **Bilingual Montessori European Project** (2023–2025) — an Erasmus+ funded initiative with partner schools in the Czech Republic, Estonia, Spain, and France, developing CI-aligned language resources specifically for Montessori settings. Worth contacting for materials and collaboration.
- **German Bilingual Montessori Schools** studied by Winnefeld (2012) — documented task-based and immersion approaches succeeding in primary Montessori contexts.

## What to expect (and what to prepare stakeholders for)

Two things to tell parents and board members up front, because they *will* come up:

**1. Accuracy will lag initially.** In year one, students taught with CI methods often make more surface errors than students drilled on grammar. This is not a problem — it's exactly what the Dutch longitudinal research (Rousse-Malpat et al.) predicted. By year two the groups equalise; by year three the CI students pull ahead in fluency and complexity. But if parents only see the year-one snapshot, they'll panic. Get ahead of this with clear communication about the research and what to expect on what timeline.

**2. It will look different than what parents remember from school.** Fewer worksheets coming home. No verb conjugation homework. Less obvious "work" in the traditional sense. This worries some parents. Counter it by sending home what students *can do* — audio recordings of them telling stories, photos of what they've read, videos of classroom discussions. Make the progress visible in the form it actually takes.

## A realistic timeline

A full transition typically takes 2–3 years to complete. A reasonable phasing:

- **Year 1:** One or two willing teachers pilot CI methods with one or two class groups. Library built. Teacher training initiated. Other classes continue as before.
- **Year 2:** Pilot expands. Early data collected. Parents of pilot classes receive detailed communication about the approach and early outcomes. Other teachers begin training.
- **Year 3:** School-wide adoption. Assessment system fully redesigned. Ongoing professional development community established.

Trying to change everything at once usually fails. Let the early adopters succeed and let their students' outcomes recruit the rest.

# Part 4: The Short Version, for Hallway Conversations

If someone asks you to summarise this in a minute, here's the version:

> Teaching a language by explaining its grammar rules is like teaching someone to ride a bike by explaining physics. The knowledge goes into the wrong part of the brain — the part that's good at remembering facts, not the part that handles automatic skills. Forty years of research, including brain imaging, shows this clearly. What actually builds the ability to speak a language is the same thing that built your first language: lots of understandable, interesting input in real contexts. Stories, reading, conversations. We know it works: classes using these methods consistently match or outperform traditional ones on every measure, while students enjoy it more and stick with it longer. And it aligns with everything else we already believe about how children learn.
